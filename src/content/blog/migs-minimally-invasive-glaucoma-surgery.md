Glaucoma surgery has historically been dominated by two procedures — trabeculectomy and tube shunt implantation — both of which are highly effective but carry meaningful risks and require considerable post-operative management. Over the past decade, a new generation of surgical approaches has emerged under the umbrella term **minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS)**, offering a more favourable risk-benefit profile for appropriately selected patients.

## What Makes a Procedure "MIGS"?

The term MIGS describes a heterogeneous group of procedures that share several characteristics:

- An **ab interno approach** (from the inside of the eye, without an external bleb)
- **Minimal disruption** to normal anatomy
- **Short operating time** and rapid recovery
- A **favourable safety profile** compared to traditional filtration surgery
- Typically performed in combination with cataract extraction

The trade-off is that MIGS procedures generally achieve more modest IOP reductions than trabeculectomy, making them most appropriate for mild to moderate glaucoma rather than advanced disease requiring very low target pressures.

## The Main MIGS Categories

### Trabecular Bypass Devices

**iStent inject W** and **Hydrus Microstent** are the most widely used CE-marked devices in this category. Implanted through the trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal, they create a permanent bypass for aqueous outflow.

The HORIZON trial demonstrated that Hydrus implantation at the time of cataract surgery provided sustained IOP reduction and significantly reduced medication burden over five years compared to cataract surgery alone.

### Goniotomy and Trabeculotomy

**Gonioscopy-assisted transluminal trabeculotomy (GATT)** uses a suture or illuminated microcatheter to open the trabecular meshwork over 360°. It is particularly effective in younger patients and those with secondary glaucomas where outflow obstruction is at the level of the meshwork.

### Subconjunctival and Suprachoroidal Devices

The **XEN Gel Stent** creates a controlled aqueous drainage pathway from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space, offering IOP reductions approaching those of trabeculectomy with a more predictable wound healing profile. The **CyPass Microstent** (now withdrawn from the market due to late corneal safety concerns) and emerging suprachoroidal devices target the uveoscleral outflow pathway.

## Combining MIGS with Cataract Surgery

One of the most clinically valuable aspects of MIGS is the opportunity to address both cataract and glaucoma in a single surgical episode. For patients with mild to moderate glaucoma undergoing routine phacoemulsification, adding an iStent inject or Hydrus adds minimal operative time, does not significantly increase risk, and can meaningfully reduce post-operative drop burden.

NICE has appraised several of these combinations and considers them cost-effective options within the NHS pathway.

## Who Benefits Most?

MIGS is most likely to benefit patients who:

- Have **mild to moderate open-angle glaucoma** with IOP inadequately controlled on maximum tolerated medical therapy
- Are **already planned for cataract surgery**
- Wish to reduce their **dependence on multiple daily drops**
- Are **not suitable candidates** for or wish to avoid the risks of trabeculectomy

For patients with advanced glaucoma, very low target pressures, or previous failed filtration surgery, traditional procedures still offer the most reliable and profound IOP reduction.

## The Evolving Landscape

MIGS is one of the most rapidly evolving areas in ophthalmology. New devices enter clinical trials regularly, and long-term data continue to accumulate. The challenge for surgeons and patients alike is navigating the evidence base critically — distinguishing robust, peer-reviewed data from industry-sponsored short-term trials.

If you have glaucoma and are considering cataract surgery, it is well worth asking your ophthalmologist whether a combined MIGS procedure might be appropriate for your situation.
