Intraocular pressure has been the central parameter in glaucoma management for over a century — and for good reason. It remains the only modifiable risk factor proven to slow the disease. Yet IOP measurement is more complex than it appears, and our understanding of why some patients progress despite controlled pressures while others remain stable with seemingly high IOPs has been fundamentally changed by the study of corneal biomechanics.

## Why Standard IOP Measurements Can Mislead

The gold standard for IOP measurement — Goldmann applanation tonometry — assumes a standard corneal thickness, curvature, and stiffness. Deviation from these assumptions introduces systematic error.

Central corneal thickness (CCT) was the first well-characterised confounder. The Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study demonstrated that thin corneas were an independent predictor of glaucoma conversion, beyond any artefactual IOP effect. This prompted widespread adoption of pachymetry in glaucoma clinics.

However, CCT alone explains only part of the variance. Two eyes with identical thickness can behave very differently under mechanical load — and it is this **biomechanical response**, rather than thickness per se, that appears to carry the greatest clinical relevance.

## Corneal Hysteresis: A Measure of Viscoelasticity

The Ocular Response Analyser (ORA, Reichert) measures **corneal hysteresis (CH)** — the difference between the inward and outward applanation pressures during an air-puff — as a surrogate for the eye's viscoelastic energy absorption. A highly viscous eye absorbs and dissipates mechanical energy efficiently; a stiffer, less damped eye transmits more energy to the optic nerve head with each pulsatile IOP fluctuation.

Lower corneal hysteresis has been associated in multiple independent studies with:

- **Faster rates of visual field progression** in established glaucoma
- **Greater structural loss** on OCT, independent of mean IOP
- **Higher risk of progression** in ocular hypertensive patients
- **Worse prognosis** after trabeculectomy

Critically, these associations persist after adjusting for CCT and mean IOP, suggesting that CH captures independent mechanobiological information about the eye's susceptibility to glaucomatous injury.

## The Corvis ST and Stiffness Parameters

The Corvis ST (Oculus) takes a complementary approach, using ultra-high-speed Scheimpflug imaging to capture the cornea's full dynamic deformation response to an air puff in three dimensions. From this, biomechanically corrected IOP (bIOP) and the **Stress-Strain Index (SSI)** — a measure of corneal stiffness independent of IOP and thickness — can be derived.

Emerging data suggest SSI may improve the detection of forme fruste keratoconus (important before refractive surgery), but its clinical utility in glaucoma risk stratification is still being evaluated in prospective studies.

## Clinical Implications: Adjusting Target IOP

The practical implication of this research is straightforward in principle: patients with low corneal hysteresis may require lower target IOPs to prevent progression than patients with high hysteresis, even when presenting with similar baseline pressures and visual field status.

Some centres now use CH to guide target IOP decisions, particularly for patients who are progressing despite apparently well-controlled pressures. A formula-based approach incorporating CH into IOP interpretation (the corneal-compensated IOP, or IOPcc, from the ORA) is available, though not yet universally adopted in clinical guidelines.

## What Remains Uncertain

Several important questions await answers from ongoing prospective research:

- Can treating to a CH-adjusted IOP target measurably improve outcomes compared to standard pressure targets?
- Is CH itself modifiable — for example, by IOP-lowering surgery or scleral tissue changes?
- What is the relationship between systemic connective tissue diseases (such as Ehlers-Danlos syndrome) and glaucoma risk via biomechanical pathways?

## A Shifting Paradigm

The study of corneal and scleral biomechanics is part of a broader shift in glaucoma research — away from a purely pressure-centric model and towards a more nuanced understanding of optic nerve head susceptibility. Factors including cerebrospinal fluid pressure, lamina cribrosa morphology, and vascular autoregulation are all active areas of investigation.

For patients, the key message is that a single IOP reading in clinic is one data point among many. Comprehensive glaucoma assessment — including structural OCT, visual field testing, corneal properties, and careful longitudinal review — is essential for accurate risk stratification and personalised management.
