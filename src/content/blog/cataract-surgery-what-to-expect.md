Cataract surgery is the most frequently performed elective surgical procedure in the NHS, with approximately 400,000 operations carried out in England each year. It is also one of the most effective — restoring functional vision in the vast majority of patients, with an excellent safety record. Despite this, many patients approach the procedure with understandable anxiety. This guide aims to demystify the process.

## What Is a Cataract?

The natural lens of the eye sits behind the iris and pupil, focusing light onto the retina. It is normally transparent. With age — and accelerated by factors such as smoking, steroid use, and UV exposure — the lens proteins gradually denature, causing progressive clouding. This is a cataract.

Symptoms include:
- Blurred or hazy vision that spectacles do not correct
- Increased glare, particularly from oncoming headlights at night
- Colours appearing washed out or yellowed
- Monocular double vision (diplopia in one eye only)
- Frequent changes in spectacle prescription

## When Should Surgery Be Considered?

Surgery is indicated when the cataract is causing sufficient visual impairment to affect your daily activities — reading, driving, recognising faces — and when no other cause of visual loss has been identified. There is no advantage to waiting until the cataract is "ripe" or very dense; modern phacoemulsification handles most degrees of lens density equally well.

## Pre-operative Assessment

Before surgery you will attend a pre-assessment clinic where:
- Your eye is measured using optical biometry (IOLMaster or Lenstar) to calculate the power of the intraocular lens (IOL) to be implanted
- You discuss any co-existing eye conditions (dry eye, macular degeneration, glaucoma) that may affect your outcome
- You choose your target refraction — typically aiming for distance vision, with reading glasses for near tasks; premium multifocal or extended-depth-of-focus lenses are also available
- Anaesthetic options are discussed (topical drops or local injection)

## The Operation Itself

Cataract surgery under the NHS is performed as a day case, usually under topical anaesthesia — anaesthetic eye drops that numb the eye surface without any injection or sedation. The procedure takes 15–20 minutes:

1. A small self-sealing incision (2.2–2.8 mm) is made at the corneal periphery
2. The front capsule of the lens is opened (capsulorhexis)
3. Ultrasound energy (phacoemulsification) fragments the cataract, which is aspirated from the eye
4. The posterior lens capsule is left intact as a scaffold
5. A foldable artificial intraocular lens is inserted through the small incision and unfolds inside the eye

No sutures are typically required. You will remain awake throughout but should feel no pain — pressure, touch, and light are normal sensations during the procedure.

## After Surgery

Recovery is rapid. Most patients notice improved vision within 24–48 hours, with full stabilisation over 4–6 weeks as the eye heals and any mild inflammation settles.

**Post-operative instructions:**
- Use prescribed antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops for 4 weeks as directed
- Avoid rubbing the eye
- Do not swim or use a hot tub for 2 weeks
- Driving is permissible once your operated eye meets the DVLA vision standard — your surgeon will advise
- Avoid strenuous exercise or heavy lifting for the first week

## Possible Complications

Cataract surgery is very safe, with a serious complication rate of approximately 1 in 1,000. Potential complications include:

- **Posterior capsule rupture** — the most common intraoperative complication (~0.5%), usually manageable
- **Posterior capsule opacification (PCO)** — secondary clouding of the capsule occurring months to years later; treated with a simple outpatient YAG laser procedure
- **Endophthalmitis** — infection inside the eye, rare but vision-threatening; report any sudden pain, redness, or vision loss urgently
- **Cystoid macular oedema** — swelling at the retinal centre, usually resolving with additional drops

## A Note on Lens Choice

Standard monofocal lenses provide excellent distance vision and are fully funded by the NHS. Premium lenses — multifocal, trifocal, or extended-depth-of-focus (EDOF) — aim to reduce dependence on glasses for both distance and near tasks but carry a small risk of halos and glare, and are not suitable for everyone (particularly those with macular disease or irregular corneas). They involve an additional patient co-payment. Your surgeon will help you determine whether a premium lens is appropriate for your visual needs and ocular health.

If you are concerned about your vision or would like to discuss whether cataract surgery is appropriate for you, please speak to your GP for an onward referral to your local ophthalmology service.
