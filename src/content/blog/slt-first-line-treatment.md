For decades, the standard approach to newly diagnosed open-angle glaucoma or ocular hypertension was to start with daily eye drops and escalate to laser or surgery only if drops failed. A landmark clinical trial has turned that convention on its head — and the implications for patients are significant.

## The LiGHT Trial

The Laser in Glaucoma and Ocular Hypertension (LiGHT) trial, led by Professor Gus Gazzard at Moorfields Eye Hospital and published in *The Lancet* in 2019, randomised 718 patients with newly diagnosed open-angle glaucoma or ocular hypertension to either selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT) or conventional eye drop therapy as their first treatment.

At three years, the results were striking:

- **93% of SLT-treated eyes** required no additional drops to control pressure, compared with far lower rates in the drops-first group
- SLT was **non-inferior** to drops in achieving target IOP
- Patients in the SLT group reported **significantly better quality of life** — freedom from the daily burden of instilling drops, avoidance of ocular surface side effects, and lower out-of-pocket costs
- The SLT group generated **lower healthcare costs** over the trial period
- **No serious adverse events** were attributable to SLT

## What Is SLT?

Selective laser trabeculoplasty is a brief, painless outpatient procedure performed at the slit lamp. A low-energy laser is applied to the trabecular meshwork — the drainage tissue at the angle of the eye — in a series of spots around 180° or 360° of the angle. The laser selectively targets pigmented cells without causing collateral thermal damage to surrounding tissue.

The procedure typically takes less than five minutes per eye. Patients may experience mild discomfort or a temporary IOP spike immediately afterwards, but serious complications are rare. Crucially, SLT can be safely repeated if its effect diminishes over time — a significant advantage over some surgical alternatives.

## Updated Clinical Guidelines

Following the LiGHT trial, the Royal College of Ophthalmologists updated its guidelines to recommend SLT as a first-line treatment option for newly diagnosed ocular hypertension and open-angle glaucoma. NICE has incorporated this evidence into its technology appraisal guidance (TA797), and SLT is now routinely offered within the NHS as an alternative to commencing drops.

## Who Is SLT Suitable For?

SLT works best in patients with:

- Open angles (confirmed on gonioscopy)
- Mild to moderate pigmentation of the trabecular meshwork
- Newly diagnosed disease where the aim is to avoid or delay drops entirely

It is less predictable in patients with very advanced glaucoma requiring very low target pressures, or in those with certain secondary glaucomas such as pseudoexfoliation — though it can still be effective in these groups.

## What Patients Should Know

If you have been recently diagnosed with glaucoma or ocular hypertension, it is worth having a conversation with your ophthalmologist about SLT as a starting treatment. The evidence supports it as safe and effective, with the practical advantage of freeing you from daily drop instillation — at least for several years.

For patients already on drops, the question of whether to switch to or supplement with SLT is more nuanced and depends on your IOP, visual field status, and individual circumstances. This is best discussed at your clinic appointment.

The key message from LiGHT is simple: in the right patient, the laser-first approach is not a compromise — it is often the better option.
