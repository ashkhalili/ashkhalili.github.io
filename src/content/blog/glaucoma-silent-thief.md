Glaucoma is one of the leading causes of irreversible blindness worldwide, affecting an estimated 76 million people globally — a figure projected to rise to 112 million by 2040. Despite this scale, it remains largely silent in its early stages: most patients lose a significant portion of their peripheral vision before they notice anything is wrong. This is why glaucoma has earned its sobriquet — the silent thief of sight.

## What Is Glaucoma?

Glaucoma is not a single disease but a family of conditions characterised by progressive damage to the optic nerve — the cable that carries visual information from the eye to the brain. In the vast majority of cases this damage is driven by elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), though a significant minority of patients develop glaucomatous damage at statistically normal pressures, a condition known as normal-tension glaucoma.

The most common form, primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG), accounts for roughly 74% of all cases in Europe. Aqueous humour — the fluid that maintains the eye's shape and nourishes its internal structures — drains too slowly through the trabecular meshwork, causing IOP to rise gradually and silently over years or decades.

## Who Is at Risk?

Several well-established risk factors increase the likelihood of developing glaucoma:

- **Raised intraocular pressure** — the single most modifiable risk factor
- **Age** — prevalence rises sharply after 60
- **Family history** — a first-degree relative with glaucoma increases your risk threefold to fourfold
- **African or Caribbean ancestry** — associated with earlier onset and more aggressive disease
- **High myopia (short-sightedness)** — particularly for POAG
- **Thin central cornea** — an independent risk factor for conversion from ocular hypertension to glaucoma
- **Diabetes and hypertension** — associated with increased risk in several large cohort studies

## Why Early Detection Matters

The optic nerve contains approximately 1.2 million retinal ganglion cell axons. By the time field defects become detectable on standard automated perimetry, roughly 25–35% of these fibres may already have been lost. Earlier structural detection — using optical coherence tomography (OCT) to measure the retinal nerve fibre layer — can identify loss before functional deficits emerge, creating a crucial window for intervention.

Current NHS guidance recommends that adults over 40 with a family history of glaucoma receive a free sight test every two years. The Glaucoma Research Foundation and the Royal College of Ophthalmologists advocate for broader population-based screening, particularly in high-risk groups.

## Treatment: Lowering the Pressure

While there is currently no cure, glaucoma is highly manageable when detected early. The cornerstone of treatment is reducing IOP — even in normal-tension disease, lowering pressure slows progression.

Options include:

- **Topical eye drops** — prostaglandin analogues (e.g. latanoprost) remain the mainstay of medical therapy
- **Selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT)** — now endorsed as first-line therapy in the LiGHT trial
- **Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS)** — a rapidly expanding category of procedures offering a favourable safety profile
- **Traditional filtration surgery** — trabeculectomy or tube shunt implantation for advanced or refractory disease

## The Importance of Adherence

One of the greatest challenges in glaucoma management is adherence. Studies consistently show that 30–50% of patients do not take their drops as prescribed. The consequences can be devastating and irreversible. If you are on glaucoma medication, regular follow-up and open dialogue with your ophthalmologist about side effects or practical difficulties are essential.

If you have not had an eye examination recently — particularly if you are over 40 or have a family history of glaucoma — please arrange one. Early detection is the most effective tool we have.
